Some of the metrics that we used for these suggestions are fairly objective.

  • The year that track was released
  • Song duration, in milliseconds
  • Tempo, in beats per minute
  • Primary key of music, for instance C Major or B Flat, encoded numerically
  • Mode, a boolean classification of whether the song is in a major key or not (which is not captured explicitly by the key metric)
  • The loudness of the track, measured in decibels and averaged across the track
  • A simple ‘explicit’ boolean for whether the track passes or fails parental control filters

Other metrics are more subjective and the numbers assigned to each track are defined by Spotify using internal algorithms. We also incorporated the following data into our song comparisons.

  • 'Danceability’ describes how suitable a track is for dancing based on a combination of musical elements including tempo, rhythm stability, beat strength, and overall regularity.
  • ‘Valence’ describes the emotions conveyed by the track on a scale between cheery and positive to angry and sad.
  • ‘Acousticness’ describes where a song sits on a spectrum between acoustic and electronic.
  • ‘Instrumentalness’ defines how much of a track contains musical instruments rather than human vocals.
  • ‘Speechiness’ refers to the the portion of the song consisting of spoken word. A rap song would have a high value but a classical concerto or Tibetan Throat singing would have a low one.
  • ‘Energy’ describes a perceptual measure of the track where soft, slow, melodic songs have lower values and loud, dynamic songs have higher ones.
  • ‘Popularity’ reports a value on a scale from 0 to 100 for how well-known and frequently played a track is.
  • ‘Liveness’ predicts how likely the track recording is to have been performed for an audience, or just a recording studio.
